What Are Ultraprocessed Foods?

Walk through any supermarket and you will find shelves packed with colourful packages and bold health claims. Behind the marketing, many of these products belong to a category scientists call ultraprocessed foods — a group that has come to dominate modern diets worldwide.

The term was defined by Brazilian researcher Carlos Monteiro and his team at the University of São Paulo as part of the NOVA classification system. According to this system, ultraprocessed foods are industrial formulations made mostly or entirely from substances extracted from foods — such as oils, fats, sugar, starch, and proteins — or synthesized in laboratories. They contain little or no whole food and are filled with additives whose purpose is to make the product last longer, look more appealing, or taste irresistible.

Common examples: soft drinks, instant noodles, packaged chips, industrial bread, flavoured yoghurt, chicken nuggets, sugary breakfast cereals, energy drinks, and most fast food.

What Do They Contain?

Reading the ingredient list of a typical ultraprocessed product can be surprising. You might find 20, 30, or even 50 different components — many of which you would never find in a home kitchen. These include high-fructose corn syrup (a concentrated form of sugar linked to obesity), excessive sodium, artificial colourings, emulsifiers, thickeners, and preservatives that allow products to sit on shelves for months without spoiling.

A useful rule: If a product has more than 5 ingredients, or contains ingredients you cannot pronounce or would not use in cooking, it is very likely ultraprocessed.

Why Are They Designed to Be Hard to Stop Eating?

Food scientists spend years engineering what the industry calls the "bliss point" — the exact combination of sugar, salt, and fat that triggers the brain's reward system, releasing dopamine (the chemical associated with pleasure). A 2019 study in the journal Cell Metabolism found that people given free access to an ultraprocessed diet consumed an average of 500 more calories per day than those eating unprocessed food — not because they were hungrier, but because the food was designed to override their body's natural satiety (fullness) signals.

Health Consequences

Large-scale research has linked high consumption of these products to increased risk of type 2 diabetes, obesity, cardiovascular disease, and depression. In Mexico — our context — the situation is particularly serious: the country has one of the highest rates of type 2 diabetes in the world, and studies consistently identify ultraprocessed food consumption as a key contributing factor.

The positive side: Research shows that adolescents who learn to read nutrition labels and understand food processing make measurably healthier choices, even when their economic situation doesn't change. Awareness is a powerful tool.

📖 Quick reference — the three categories

🌱 Natural: Not industrially altered. Examples: fresh fruit, eggs, plain meat, milk, water.

🥫 Processed: Natural foods preserved by simple methods (salting, canning, fermenting). Still recognisable as food. Examples: canned beans, cheese, smoked fish, plain bread.

⚠️ Ultraprocessed: Industrial formulations with long ingredient lists, additives, and flavourings. Designed in factories, not kitchens. Examples: soft drinks, chips, instant noodles, flavoured cereals.
